Tzais 120 Minutes¶

Deprecated as a warning that this is a very late time and should be used only lechumra; using it lekula can result in chillul Shabbos and similar concerns.

Tzais, nightfall, according to a very late calculation associated with Rav Chaim Naeh, based on the view that a mil according to the Rambam is 24 minutes, and on Ula’s calculation of tzais as 5 mil after sunset.

Calculated as 120 minutes after sunset, or after sea-level sunset depending on the elevation setting.

This time is extremely late, long past the 18° point where the darkest point is reached, and should be used only lechumra, such as delaying the start of nighttime mitzvos.

The time may not be available where the calculation cannot be made, such as in the Arctic Circle where there is at least one day a year when the sun does not rise and one when it does not set.

Technical details

Source method: ComprehensiveZmanimCalendar.getTzais120Minutes

Technical reference: com.kosherjava.zmanim.ComprehensiveZmanimCalendar.getTzais120Minutes

Tzais 120 Zmanis¶

Deprecated as a warning that this is a very late time and should be used only lechumra; using it lekula can result in chillul Shabbos and similar concerns.

Tzais, dusk, according to a very late calculation of 120 zmaniyos minutes after sea-level sunset.

Calculated as 120 zmaniyos minutes after sea-level sunset.

This time is extremely late, when the sun is well below the 18° point, described as scientifically the darkest point, in most places on the globe. It should be used only lechumra, such as delaying the start of nighttime mitzvos.

The time may not be available where the calculation cannot be made, such as in the Arctic Circle where there is at least one day a year when the sun does not rise and one when it does not set.

Tzais 16 Point 1 Degrees¶

Tzais at the point when the sun is 16.1° below the horizon. This corresponds to the sun’s dip below the horizon 72 minutes after sunset according to Rabbeinu Tam’s calculation of tzais around the equinox / equilux in Jerusalem.

Calculated when the sun is 16.1° below the horizon.

The question of equinox versus equilux is complex. Rabbi Meir Posen in Ohr Meir is cited as holding that the equilux should be used, while Rabbi Yedidya Manet in Zmanei HaHalacha Lema'aseh and Rabbi Yonah Mertzbuch are cited as holding that the astronomical equinox should be used. The difference is about 9 seconds.

The time may not be available in northern and southern locations, even south of the Arctic Circle and north of the Antarctic Circle, where the sun may not reach low enough below the horizon for this calculation.

Source method: ComprehensiveZmanimCalendar.getTzais16Point1Degrees

Technical reference: com.kosherjava.zmanim.ComprehensiveZmanimCalendar.getTzais16Point1Degrees

Tzais 72 Zmanis¶

Tzais, or dusk, according to a calculation of Rabbeinu Tam’s time of tzeis as described by the Minchas Cohen in Ma’amar 2:4.

Calculated as 72 zmaniyos minutes, equal to 1/10 of the day, after sea level sunset.

This calculation makes the time from sunset to tzais shortest at the winter solstice, longest at the summer solstice, and 72 clock minutes at the equinox.

The source notes that this does not match the actual pattern of twilight, since twilight is not directly related to the length of the day.

This time may not be available when the calculation cannot be computed, such as in the Arctic Circle where there is at least one day a year when the sun does not rise and one when it does not set.

Tzais 90 Minutes¶

Tzais, or nightfall, based on the opinion of the Magen Avraham that the time to walk a mil according to the Rambam is 18 minutes, and on Ula’s calculation of tzais as 5 mil after elevation-adjusted sunset.

Calculated as 90 minutes after sunset or sea level sunset, depending on whether elevation is being used.

A similar calculation uses solar-position calculations based on this time.

This time may not be available when the calculation cannot be computed, such as in the Arctic Circle where there is at least one day a year when the sun does not rise and one when it does not set.

Tzais Ateret Torah¶

Tzais associated with Chacham Yosef Harari-Raful of Yeshivat Ateret Torah, though the source notes that he uses this time only for calculating other zmanei hayom such as Sof Zman Krias Shema and Plag Hamincha, and that his calendars do not publish a zman for Tzais.

Usually calculated as 40 minutes after sea level sunset, with the offset configurable to another number of minutes.

Chacham Harari-Raful provided a 25-minute zman for Israel.

The default described here is 40 minutes year-round in any place on the globe.

This time may not be available when the calculation cannot be computed, such as in the Arctic Circle where there is at least one day a year when the sun does not rise and one when it does not set.

Tzais Baal Hatanya¶

Tzais, or nightfall, based on the opinion of the Baal Hatanya.

Calculated when the sun is 6° below the western geometric horizon after sunset. This is based on the sun’s position about 24 minutes after sea level sunset in Jerusalem around the equinox / equilux, rounded from a calculation of about 5.83° below the horizon.

The calculation is described in relation to About Our Zmanim Calculations @ Chabad.org, using shkiah amitis as 1.583° below the horizon, calculated around the equinox / equilux.

The source describes adding 18 minutes of ¾ of a 24-minute mil and two minutes for bain hashmashos of Rav Yosi.

This time may not be available in northern and southern locations, even south of the Arctic Circle and north of the Antarctic Circle, where the sun may not reach low enough below the horizon for this calculation.

Technical details

Source method: ComprehensiveZmanimCalendar.getTzaisBaalHatanya

Technical reference: com.kosherjava.zmanim.ComprehensiveZmanimCalendar.getTzaisBaalHatanya

Tzais 72 Minutes¶

Tzais, or nightfall, based on Rabbeinu Tam’s opinion that tzeis hakochavim is 72 minutes after sunset, the time it takes to walk 4 mil at 18 minutes per mil.

Calculated as 72 standard clock minutes after sunset or sea level sunset, depending on whether elevation is being used.

According to the Machtzis Hashekel in Orach Chaim 235:3, the Pri Megadim in Orach Chaim 261:2, and others cited in the source, the 72 minutes are standard clock minutes at any time of year and in any location.

This time may not be available when the calculation cannot be computed, such as in the Arctic Circle where there is at least one day a year when the sun does not rise and one when it does not set.

Technical details

Source method: ZmanimCalendar.getTzais72Minutes
